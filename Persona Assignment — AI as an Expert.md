Happy to give you a broad overview then! Here are the key principles for pricing a new software product:

Start with value, not cost  
The most important question is: what is this worth to the customer? Cost-plus pricing (adding a margin to your costs) almost always leaves money on the table with software, since marginal costs are near zero. Instead, estimate the value you create — time saved, revenue generated, problems avoided — and price as a fraction of that.

Three frameworks to consider  
Value-based pricing — anchor to the outcome you deliver. If you save a business 10 hours/month at $100/hr, charging $200/month is an easy sell.  
Competitive pricing — if substitutes exist, your price needs to be justifiable relative to them. You can charge more if you're meaningfully better; you may need to charge less to win early customers.  
Willingness-to-pay research — ask prospects directly: "At what price would this be too expensive? Too cheap to trust? A bargain?"

A few practical rules  
Most founders underprice, especially early on. If nobody pushes back on your price, you're probably too cheap. Aim for ~20% of prospects to say it's expensive — that's a healthy signal.  
For SaaS, use freemium or free trial with clear upgrade triggers.  
For B2B, price per seat or outcome instead of flat fee.

The meta-advice  
Pick a slightly uncomfortable price and test it in the market. Pricing is iterative.

B2B SaaS Pricing Recommendations for a Pakistani Founder

Before diving in, Pakistani SaaS founders often underprice due to local market comparisons vs global value perception.

1. Anchor to value delivered, not cost  
Price based on customer value, not development cost. If your tool saves thousands in labor cost, price accordingly.

2. Use tiered pricing (Starter, Growth, Enterprise)  
Design the middle tier as the main target. It should feel like the “best value” option.

3. Push annual contracts with discounts  
Offer 15–20% discount for annual plans to improve cash flow and reduce churn.

One principle: never compete on being cheap — compete on outcomes.
