# Compound Interest - Comparison

## Step 2: What is compound interest?

**Answer:**
Compound interest is the interest you earn on interest. Unlike simple interest, which only calculates returns based on the initial amount you invest or borrow, compound interest calculates returns on your initial principal plus the interest that has already accumulated over previous periods. Because of this, your money grows at an accelerating rate—a phenomenon often called the "snowball effect."

**Example:**
Invest $1,000 at 10% annual interest for 3 years:

| Year | Simple Interest | Compound Interest |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| Year 1 | Earns $100 → Balance: $1,100 | Earns $100 → Balance: $1,100 |
| Year 2 | Earns $100 → Balance: $1,200 | Earns $110 → Balance: $1,210 |
| Year 3 | Earns $100 → Balance: $1,300 | Earns $121 → Balance: $1,331 |

**Formula:**
A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

## Step 3: Explain compound interest and give 3 concrete real-world examples with numbers.

**Answer:**
Compound interest is the interest you earn on both your original money (the principal) and the interest you've already accumulated.

**3 Real-World Examples:**

**Example 1: High-Yield Savings Account**
- Principal: $10,000
- Rate: 5% annually
- Compounding: Monthly
- Time: 10 years
- Result: $16,470.09 (earned $6,470.09 in interest)

**Example 2: Credit Card Debt**
- Principal: $5,000
- Rate: 20% annually
- Compounding: Daily
- Time: 3 years
- Result: $9,110.14 (nearly doubled the debt)

**Example 3: Long-Term Index Fund Investing**
- Principal: $20,000
- Rate: 10% annually
- Compounding: Annually
- Time: 30 years
- Result: $348,988.05
